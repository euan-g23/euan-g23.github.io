Digital Signal Acquisition & Processing (DSP Coursework)

2025 · Coursework Project

A practical coursework project focused on Data Acquisition (DA) and Digital Signal Processing (DSP), demonstrating the complete workflow from sensor-based data collection to time- and frequency-domain analysis, FFT-based spectral characterisation, and digital filter design using MATLAB.

At a Glance

  • Methods: Time-domain signal analysis, FFT, down-sampling, FIR filtering
  • Data: Bending sensor, piezoelectric force sensor, IMU
  • Tools: MATLAB, Arduino, experimental sensor data
  • Focus: Signal interpretation, spectral resolution, noise suppression

Problem Context

Engineering sensor data is often noisy, high-volume, and difficult to interpret directly in the time domain. Effective data acquisition and digital signal processing are therefore essential for extracting meaningful physical behaviour, identifying dominant frequencies, and ensuring that sampling and filtering choices do not distort the underlying signal.

Workflow

Experimental sensor signals were first collected and converted into MATLAB-compatible formats. Time-domain inspection was used to assess signal quality and suitability for further analysis. A representative bending sensor signal was then transformed into the frequency domain using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), enabling identification of dominant vibration frequencies and evaluation of sampling effects through controlled down-sampling.

Key Results

FFT analysis revealed a dominant vibration component near 20 Hz in the bending sensor data. Down-sampling the signal reduced spectral resolution and, when the Nyquist limit fell below this frequency, resulted in loss of key spectral content. A low-pass FIR filter was successfully designed and applied to suppress high-frequency noise while preserving the primary signal behaviour in both time and frequency domains.

Limitations & Lessons

The study highlighted the importance of selecting appropriate sampling rates prior to down-sampling, as inadequate Nyquist limits can lead to aliasing and information loss. The work reinforced the need for careful signal selection, spectral inspection, and justification of filter parameters when processing experimental sensor data.
