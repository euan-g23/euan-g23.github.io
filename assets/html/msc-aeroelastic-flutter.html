Dynamic Aeroelastic Flutter Modelling

2025-04 · MSc Coursework

A dynamic aeroelastic flutter model was developed for a two-dimensional aerofoil using MATLAB and Simulink, incorporating coupled plunge and pitch motion and later extended to include a trailing-edge flap. The simulations were used to identify flutter onset and assess stability sensitivity to aerodynamic and structural parameters. The work highlights both the effectiveness of reduced-order aeroelastic models and the practical challenges involved in extending them to higher-fidelity configurations.

At a Glance

  • Methods: Reduced-order aeroelastic modelling (2DOF → 3DOF)
  • Tools: MATLAB, Simulink
  • Focus: Flutter onset, stability analysis, model extension

Problem Context

Aeroelastic flutter represents a critical dynamic instability driven by the coupling between aerodynamic loading and structural response. Reduced-order models are widely used during early design stages, but their validity and limitations must be well understood when extending model fidelity or incorporating additional degrees of freedom.

Modelling Approach

The aerofoil was modelled initially as a two-degree-of-freedom system with coupled plunge and pitch motion, implemented in MATLAB and Simulink. Aerodynamic forcing was represented using linear unsteady aerodynamics, with time-domain simulations used to assess dynamic stability under varying flow conditions. The model was later extended to include a trailing-edge flap, introducing an additional degree of freedom.

Key Results

The simulations successfully identified flutter onset through divergence in time-domain responses. While the 2DOF model exhibited stable and interpretable behaviour, the introduction of a flap increased system sensitivity and highlighted numerical and formulation challenges associated with higher-order aeroelastic models.

Limitations & Engineering Lessons

The study demonstrated that while reduced-order aeroelastic models are effective for identifying flutter trends, extending them to higher-fidelity configurations requires careful formulation and validation. The work emphasised the importance of model transparency, stability diagnostics, and understanding the limits of simplified aerodynamic assumptions.
